pycon 2014 Sweden: the bad bits

I’m just back from pycon 2014 Sweden.

And all the tweets and commentary will be positive, as they always are. If you want to say something negative its best to not say it at all?

Hmm, not me.  Someone has to give this feedback; we can’t sweep it under the carpet.

First, the good bits:

James Powell’s talk on embedding CPython in CPython was … fascinating!  Whilst the whole point of the exercise is questionable, to say the least, his infectious enthusiasm, pace and style really got the audience swinging.

Kenneth Reitz’s talk on Python 2 vs Python 3 - nobody’s using Python 3, and Kenneth thinks we have to start or we’ll lose our beloved Python - was super interesting to everybody.  OK, so his actual slides were, well, hmm, silly… really, the whole evolution angle was so riddled with false analogies that the first half of the talk would have been better skipped.  But by the end he had everyone’s attention about such a sensitive and important topic that affects us all.  If <5% of users are using Python 3, just who have the Python core devs been working for these past five years?

Laurens Van Houtven’s crypto 101 talk was also fascinating.  OK, so he knows that even his claim that Twisted validates certs is questionable in the aftermath of the heartbleed mass-revocation, but I think if he can get everyone to use TLS always, we’ll stand a better chance of upgrading that when we’ve secured it (the whole CA angle, which I’ve been fascinated by for ages, seems less tractable than the cipher issues and I didn’t buy his dismissal of RC4 either) is a tad higher?

So these are the international speaker circuit.  They do these trips and give these talks because they are good at them.  But the lesson isn’t to only use tried-and-tested international speakers, because all the lightning talk slots were fascinating too.  The audience was technical, and when the audience put themselves forward to present, they had something to say that was interesting to the audience.  The audience were full of good potential future talks for next year.

And the first keynote, by Helena Bengtsson, was interesting too.  Nothing python-related really, and she only talked about the why not the how, but investigative journalism is interesting and the audience enjoyed it.

Still good:

Isaac Bernat’s talk was a good talk but could have had more how and less why.  Questions got a bit caught up in the need to abort builds if there are TODOs in them, which is a shame because there’d have been a lot of good talking to be had about how he pipes together things in bash, and perhaps why he doesn’t use multiprocessing to pipe together tools and filters instead.

And Beatrice Åkerblom’s research into dynamic languages and counting the occurrences of dynamic usage in a Python corpus was interesting.  The subject matter deeply interests me so I perhaps paid closer attention than others.  He delivery was a bit non-committal, and if she’s been at it since SourceForge was mainstream then I hope she’s been busy with other things because she showed what you can get from a few days effort.  Her results also surprised me, but there’s a few things I’ve learned from my attempts at statifying Python that might mislead results, namely the duckability of truthiness, collections and so on, and the re-use of variables that, when thought about using SSTA, goes away.

Attendees are doubtless tying to work out by my omissions what I thought was bad…

The Bad bits:

A couple of talks simply weren’t right.

I’m not sure why Jack Palmer came to talk about his startup plot.ly but he sure did just read the slides and drown the audience in apathy.  He brightened up and acted sharp to questions, so he’s got passion for the subject deep down, but it completely hid during his main presentation.  A shame.  Not everyone is a born presenter.

The worst though was Jacqueline Kazil.  I mean, what on earth was her point?  I trust our ticket money didn’t fund her trip!  And after a sequence of disjoint incomplete slides - the only highlights being photos she’d inserted of other people’s talks - she goes and finishes super early.  No questions from the audience… so there you go, I think she got the message.

But the worst bit?

The waiting around.  The schedule had absolutely massive wastelands of waiting.  The lunch break alone was scheduled for 2 hours.  And when Kazil finished super early, there was even a wait for the food.

Programmers are not a social bunch, and not good at smalltalk small talk.  Those that came with buddies clustered around them and talked to their buddies.  Those that travelled alone stood alone, waiting.  Everyone had their arms crossed and even the organizers - programmers, obviously - exhibited this behaviour.

And I don’t think you can really blame us programmers for this.  We aren’t going to get a crash course on polite conversation with strangers simply by having to endure two hour waits.

Kenneth Reitz’s audience polling showed that the audience were firmly experienced programmers and really none of the “Python is my first language” people that - apparently, I haven’t met them - exist.  There was very little scientist angle, and very all the journalists were speakers not audience.  So know the audience!  The audience wanted pure uncensored programming, and didn’t really get it.  The just got long awkward waits. 

The lightning talks showed the crowd was full of people with interesting things to share and say.  The lightning talks, if they’d allowed for questions, would have been excellent at four times the allotted time.

If the schedule had been over full and over technical, and everyone rushing and hurrying, I think the buzz would have built and then programmers would have started getting social.

I have a dirty little secret: when faced with a 150 minute wait on the second day after Kazil’s piss poor talk, I just waited long enough for the baguettes to arrive before strolling off into the Stockholm sunshine and going to see the town.  Stockholm is a super-lovely pretty town, with a super-safe feeling as you wander around.  Well recommend visiting.  If you’ve never seen the Vasa, you really really really should.

I’d have liked to see the Machine Learning talk, but I’ve already seen it on youtube from the main pycon, and it finished at 6 which would have been hard on the airport journey, so I reluctantly skipped it.  My feedback would be “please give the same talk next year, but use Collaborative Filtering” ;)

So, next year…

Much more technical talks.  No real breaks.  Much more rushing around and bustle.  If everyone complains they couldn’t see it all, and that they finished exhausted, that’s a good conference!
